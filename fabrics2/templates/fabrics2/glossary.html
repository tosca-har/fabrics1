Plane Polarised Light (PPL) and Cross Polarised Light (XPL)

Microscopes used for petrography have a polariser positioned between the light source and the slide, and an analyser (a second polariser, rotated perpendicularly to the first), between the slide and eye-piece. The polariser restricts the light entering the sample to that oscillating/vibrating in a single plane/direction. Minerals in the sample can split the plane light into 2 rays of different speeds. The analyser resolves the two rays. Under PPL conditions, the analyser is removed and so is not in use. The effect of the polariser alone, is some decreased light intensity and some change of colour (pleochroism) for some minerals, as a slide is rotated. Under XPL conditions, the analyser is present and the difference (birefringence) in how the 2 rays were refracted by the sample is visible in the interference colour of the crystals. How the the rays are refracted depends on both crystal characteristics, the orientation of the crystal and the thickness of the section. As the crystal orientation is changed with rotation of the slide, at a particular angle the 2 rays will be refracted at the same speed. This refraction will cause the rays to destructively interfere with each other as they recombine in the analyser. This results in the crystal appearing black. The angle at which this occurs (relative to the crystal direction) is known as the 'extinction' angle. The maximum extinction angle is a distinguishing characteristic of a mineral. See Perkins (2022) section 5, Labotka (2014) or Nelson (2017).

The components of Pacific pottery fabrics

Calcareous

Calcareous (calcium carbonate in calcite or aragonite grains) fabrics include those from reef debris and those from limeclasts (fragments of limestone).

While limestones are sedimentary rocks, they were generally, included in 'calcareous' by Dickinson, and so have been here. Beach ("white") sand, with the bioclasts ('skeletons') of minute reef organisms (such as foraminifera), was a commonly used temper. For many Pacific atoll communities it was the only one available. Most Pacific sites feature at least one calcareous or hybrid calcareous temper. Unfortunately this makes reef debri calcareous tempers hard to source to location. However their presence at an inland site is evidence of pottery (or at least temper) importation. In contrast, limeclast fabrics may derive from interior eroded limestones and thus can potentially be more diagnostic. Limeclasts can be distinguished from bioclasts by their unordered internal structure, although the possibility of degraded bioclasts is a confounding factor. Limeclast fabrics are found in Timor (tpr200), the Marianas (tpr4), Vanuatu (tpr249) and Fiji (e.g. tpr373).

Calcareous inclusions are light grey in PPL and XPL (where they show pale higher order colours). See Dickinson (2006) pg 3, 20, 23, Strekeisen (2006-2020) calcite, carbonate rocks, or Perkins, (2022) section 7.4.3.

Quartz

Quartz (silcon dioxide) does not occur ubiquitously in Pacific geology and so is an important distinguishing mineral in the Pacific tempers. It is a dominant mineral in dissected orogen and quartzose variant tectonic highland tempers from up-lifted geology, and also occurs (generally in lower proportions) in the dacitic andesitic arc tempers. Quartzose tempers are found in pottery from New Caledonia (e.g. tpr296), Maluku (e.g. tpr80), PNG (e.g. tpr270), Carolines (e.g. tpr42), Fiji (e.g. tpr388), Australia (e.g. tpr204), Tonga (e.g. tpr437) and the Marianas (e.g. tpr10). In the Marianas they are indicative of Saipan, the main island with dacitic rocks.

Polycrystalline aggregates of quartz, derived from quartz veins were considered with the monocrystalline quartz, as 'quartz', while sedimentary chert or metamorphic quartzite were classed as sedimentary/metasedimentary rock fragments.

Quartz minerals are white in PPL and white, grey or extinct (black) in XPL (i.e. first order colours). Quartz from plutonic igneous rocks, often have undulose (wavy) extinction, where the extinction travels across the crystal as the polarisation angle changes. Quartz crystals resistance to alteration or degradation (compared to feldspars) can also be a diagnostic feature. See Dickinson (2006) pg 23, 27, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.2,14.1.1.1.

Feldspars

Feldspars (aluminium tectosilicates) found in Pacific tempers include the plagioclases and the K-feldspars. Feldspars being the most common mineral, feldspathic dominant tempers occur in all regions, including the Marianas (tpr13), Solomons (tpr110), Bismarcks (tpr198), New Caledonia (tpr308), Carolines (tpr210), Vanuatu (tpr233) and Fiji (tpr406). While plagioclase are often one of the dominant minerals in all the non-calcareous temper types (andesitic arc, post-arc cover, oceanic basalt, tectonic highland and dissected orogen), the K-feldspars only dominate in the dissected orogen.

Feldspars are white in PPL and have black, white and grey coloured 'twinning' in XPL. Plagioclase twinning often appears as black and white stripes, while K-feldspars have more simple or cross-hatched twinning. See Dickinson (2006) pg 23, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.3, 14.1.1.2.

Amphiboles

The most common of the amphiboles (ferromagnesian double chain (ino-) silicates) in Pacific tempers are hornblende, oxy-hornblende, glaucophane and cummingtonite. Hornblendes are found in high levels in fabrics from the Solomons (tpr143), Bismarcks (tpr197), PNG (tpr181), Batanes (tpr58), Banda (Maluku, tpr67), Fiji (tpr357) and Eua (Tonga, tpr437). Oxy-hornblendes are generally found at lower levels than hornblendes in some fabrics, but are at higher levels in particular sherds of tpr348 from Kadavu, Fiji. Glaucophane and cummingtonite are found in certain New Caledonian fabrics (tpr230 and tpr303).

Hornblendes are green (or yellow to brown) in PPL, with several Solomons fabrics being distinguishable by their yellow-brown (tpr111) and green (tpr121) hornblendes. Oxy-hornblende and cummingtonite are brown and glaucophane is light blue-violet in PPL. Amphiboles have bright (2nd order) colours in XPL and extinction angles of around 20°. They have 2 cleavage lines at 56° and 124°. See Dickinson (2006) pg 25, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.8, 14.1.3.2.

Pyroxenes

The most common pyroxenes (ferromagnesian single chain (ino-) silicates) in Pacific tempers are the clinopyroxene (with monoclinic crystals) auguite, although orthopyroxenes (with orthorhombic crystals), such as hypersthene, also occurs. Pyroxenic fabrics are found in all regions, including Fiji (e.g. tpr334), Tonga (e.g. tpr438), Marianas (e.g. tpr17), Vanuatu (e.g. tpr165), Solomons (e.g. tpr173), PNG (e.g. tpr274), Bismarcks (e.g. tpr317), Samoa (e.g. tpr292), New Caledonia (e.g. tpr307), Carolines (e.g. tpr52) and Marquesa (e.g. tpr441). Orthopyroxenes are found (with clinopyroxenes) in Tongan fabrics (tpr432), New Britain (Bismarcks, tpr192), Saipan (Marianas, tpr13) and Vanua Levu (Fiji, tpr386).

Pyroxenes are white (or very lightly green) in PPL and brightly coloured (2nd order colours) in XPL. They have 2 cleavage lines at 90° and 180°. While clinopyroxenes have maximum extinction at 40°, orthopyroxenes are at 0° (parallel). See Dickinson (2006) pg 25, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.7, 14.1.3.1.

Opaque iron oxides

Opaque iron oxides in Pacific fabrics are predominantly magnetite (iron oxide spinel), with also ilmenite (iron-titanium oxide) occurring. While magnetite and ilmenite are derived from igneous or metamorphic rocks, in some situations, sedimentary rock (reworked iron-rich nodules) derived contribution occur in the form of the iron oxides/hydroxides, limonite and hematite. Most regions have a fabric, often a placer sand, with high levels of opaques, including Solomons (tpr156), Bismarcks (tpr194), Vanuatu (tpr266), Fiji(tpr413), New Caledonia ( tpr304), Tonga ( tpr427), Marquesa (tpr441), and PNG (tpr300).

Opaques appear both black in PPL and XPL. With reflected light microscopy, magnetite and ilemnite appear gray, limonite appears white, and hematite appears bright red. See Dickinson (2006) pg 22, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 14.5.1, 14.5.2,14.6.

Olivine

Olivine (ferromagnesian nesosilicate) is found in some Pacific fabrics, primarily those derived from basalts. It is found less in fabrics, than in their sources, as it is more susceptible to degration than other minerals (such as pyroxenes). Fabrics with olivines come from Samoa (tpr295), Solomons (tpr154), Fiji (tpr392), Erromango (Vanuatu, tpr222a) and TLTF chain (Bismarcks, tpr134). With lower levels in fabrics from Pohnpei (Carolines, tpr210), Tonga (tpr436 ) and Marquesas (tpr441).

Olivines are white to pale green/brown in PPL and have bright (2nd order) colours in XPL. Their similar colours to pyroxenes can make them harder to distinguish, but they only have poor cleavage, and have higher relief. Extinction is at 0° (parallel). See Dickinson (2006) pg 26, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.10,14.1.5.2.

Mica

Micas (aluminium phyllosilicates) found in Pacific tempers, include muscovite and the ferromagnesian (incorporates iron and magnesium) biotite . They are found in tempers in the Carolines (tpr272), Maluku (tpr80) and PNG (tpr271), and at lower levels in other andesitic arc and dissected orogen tempers.

Exposure to heated liquids (such as those from cooling magmas) can alter feldspars to the mica sericite .

Muscovite often has a flaky, irrelgular apparance, is white in PPL and has higher order (bright electric-like) colours in XPL. Biotite, commonly has a rectangular appearance, is brown (or dark green) in PPL, and has higher order colors or is dark brown in XPL. It goes extinct (black) when its long direction is parallel to the x or y axis. Sericite alterations appear as a golden colour in PPL and XPL. See Dickinson (2006) pg 26, Strekeisen (2006-2020) muscovite, biotite, sericite or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.5, 14.1.2.3.

Epidote

Epidotes (calcium ferro-aluminium sorosilicates), are generally found in Pacific fabrics, in polycrystalline aggregates derived from rock alteration (metamorphic or hydrothermal). They are found in PNG (tpr91), Fiji (tpr332), Vanuatu(tpr247), New Caledonia (tpr299), Bismarcks (tpr133), Solomons (tpr138), Cagayan (Philippines, tpr55) and Carolines (tpr42)

Epidotes are clear to light yellow-green in PPL, and very brightly coloured (higher order) in XPL. They have 1 cleavage line at 25-40° and extinction is at 0° (parallel) when measured relative to the length of the crystal (not the cleavage line). See Dickinson (2006) pg 26, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) sections 6.4.9, 14.1.6.

Garnet and Red Chrome Spinel

Minerals found in Pacific fabrics, that are nonopaque (translucent) but isotropic, include garnets (a nesosilicate mineral group) and red chrome spinel (a non-silicate of the spinel group). In Pacific fabrics garnets are not found in abundances above 5%, but occur in fabrics from Buru (Maluku, tpr69) and New Caledonia (tpr296). Red chrome spinels occur in selected fabrics from PNG (tpr183) and New Caledonia (tpr311).

Garnets are clear in PPL, while red chrome spinels are a dark red colour. Both are black in XPL . See Dickinson (2006) pg 25, Strekeisen (2006-2020) garnet, or Perkins, (2022) sections 14.1.5.1, 14.5.2.

Igneous Rock Fragments

Note that for the abundances reported in this database, igneous and metavolcanic rocks are reported together.

Igneous rocks are derived from crytallised magma. Igneous rocks are classified by crystal size, which is dependent on magma cooling time. In volcanic rocks, the magma cooled on the earths surface (i.e. lava) results in an aphanitic (very fine) texture, whereas the slower cooling underground, in plutonic rocks results in larger crystals (ie a phaneritic texture). The different proportions of K-feldspars, quartz, plagioclase, pyroxenes, olivines, micas and amphiboles, give rise to different rock types. The most common volcanic rock types in Pacific tempers include andesites, basalts and dacites, with some rhyolites. The more common plutonic rock types include diorites, granodiorites, and gabbro, with some granites and peridotites.

Woudloper, Public domain, via Wikimedia Commons

All regions contain at least one fabric variant dominated by igneous lithic fragments, including Vanuatu (tpr250), Marianas (tpr20), Samoa (tpr293a), Bismarck (tpr180), Fiji (tpr410), Tonga (tpr374) and PNG (tpr88). Igneous volcanic rock textures include vitric, felsitic, microlitic, and lathwork. For plutonic rocks, microphaneritic is used for fragments with smaller crystals, like the hypabassal and microdiorites , while plutonic is used here for larger crystals. However, it is important to note that the distinction between the different textures is operator subjective, and should be taken as a guide only and refer to fabric images where possible. See Dickinson (2006) pg 22, 26, Strekeisen (2006-2020) plutonic, volcanic, or Perkins, (2022) section 6.

Vitric

Vitric (glass) fragments are traditionally included as igneous rock fragments, as they derive from cooled magma, sometimes from pyroclastic debri. The colour of the glass varies from brown or red (from mafic sources, more rich in metals like iron and magnesium) to tan or colourless (from felsic, less ferromagnesian sources). Vitric fragments from volcanic ash can show curvilinear and branching, and are generally clear. Vitric fragments from pyroclastic flows have eutaxitic textures with elongated vesicles. Vitric fragments with more rounded vesicles have a pumiceous texture. Fabrics with vitrics come from Vanuatu (tpr233), Marianas (tpr25), Samoa (tpr294), Bismarcks (tpr119), Tonga (tpr374) and Carolines (tpr211).

If crystals occur within the vitric fragment, they can be described as vitrophyric. See Dickinson (2006) pg 21, 26, 50, 110, or Strekeisen (2006-2020) glass, pumice, vitrophyric.

Felsitic

Felsitic fragments contain crystals with irregular shapes. They are commonly contain plagioclase microphenocrysts in a mosaic microcrystalline quartz-feldspar groundmass formed from devitrified glass. Felsitic fragments can have granular or more seriated terxtures. Fabrics containing felsitic fragments occur in most regions, particularly those with andesitic or dacitic sources including Vanuatu (tpr250), Carolines (tpr250), Maluku (tpr190), Samoa (tpr293a), Solomons (tpr144), Bismarcks (tpr179), Fiji (tpr343a) and Tonga (tpr209). See Dickinson (2006) pg 27, or Strekeisen (2006-2020) felsitic.

Microlitic

Microlitic fragments contain (usually plagioclase) crystals with more regular, generally elongated shapes. They can be pilotaxitic (more packed), or hyalopilitic (more spaced out in glass). They are found primarily in andesitic and basaltic andesites in regions including Vanuatu (tpr248), Solomons (tpr172), Samoa (tpr293b), Bismarcks (tpr178), Futuna (tpr325) and Fiji (tpr344). See Dickinson (2006) pg 27, or Strekeisen (2006-2020) hyalopilitic, pilotaxitic.

Lathwork

Lathwork fragments contain larger (often plagioclase) crystals. They can be intersertal (set in glass) or intergranular (with other crystals such as pyroxenes). They are found primarily in basalt or basanite fabrics in regions including Samoa (tpr294b), Uvea (tpr328) and Fiji (tpr332). See Dickinson (2006) pg 27, or Strekeisen (2006-2020) intergranular, intersertal.

Microphaneritic

Lithics with Dickinson often used the term 'microphaneritic' to include the hypabassal to plutonic spectrum of fragments containg crystals ranging in size. Hypabassal fragments characterise fabrics from Solomons (tpr127) and other microphaneritic fabrics include those from Vanuatu (tpr254). Larger plutonic fragments are found in fabrics from PNG (tpr270), Bismarcks (tpr314) and the Solomons (tpr276). See Dickinson (2006) pg 26, Strekeisen (2006-2020) plutonic, or Perkins, (2022) section 14.1.2.3.

Metavolcanic

Note that for the abundances reported in this database, igneous and metavolcanic rocks are reported together.

Metavolcanics are igneous rocks that have been altered by high pressures and or temperatures, giving them a foliated appearance. In Pacific fabrics the metavolcanics found include actinolite-chlorite-albite greenschists, ultramafic serpentinites and amphibole-plagioclase amphibolites. Source regions for metavolcanic fabrics include Carolines (tpr272 with greenschist) and New Caledonia (tpr303, featuring amphibolite with feldspar-cummingtonite and tpr310, featuring serpentinite). See Dickinson (2006) pg 27, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) section 8.

Sedimentary and Metasedimentary

Sedimentary rocks are formed by accumulation/deposition. Metasedimentary rocks are formed when sedimentary rocks are altered by high pressure or temperature and generally have striated crystals. Sedimentary rocks in Pacific fabrics, as per Dickinson, include chert (quartz microcrylstalline aggregates), siltstone (quartz-rich) and argillite (shale or mudrock). Metasedimentary include quartzite (quartz polycrylstalline aggregates) and tectonite (slate and phyllite).

Regions with sedimenary and metasedimentary fabrics include Buru (Maluku tpr69), New Caldedonia (tpr299), Carolines (tpr41), PNG (tpr88), Fiji (tpr343a) and the Philippines (tpr57). See Dickinson (2006) pg 27, Strekeisen (2006-2020) sedimentary, phyllite, quartzite or Perkins, (2022) section 7.

Grog

In the Pacific, the use of broken/crushed pottery (grog) as temper is mainly limited to the West Carolines (e.g. tpr39) and PNG (tpr179a). See Dickinson (2006) pg 21.

Rock types

Basaltic

Basalts are igneous (volcanic) rocks from lava (surface magma) that cooled quickly, above (or near) the earth's surface. The quick cooling leads to fine crystal grains. Basalts have less silca (i.e. less quartz) than andesites, and contain minerals such as plagioclase feldspars, clinopyroxene (and orthopyroxene) and olivine. These minerals sometimes occur as larger crystal laths, formed before the lava reached the surface. Rock fragments described as basaltic, may also include basanites and the more felsic hawaiite, as they can be difficult to distinguish visually. Basaltic tempers are found in regions such as the East Carolines (e.g. tpr211), Samoa (e.g. tpr294a) and Uvea (e.g. tpr328). See Dickinson (2006) pg 30, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) section 6.6.

Andesitic

Andesites are igneous (volcanic) rocks that have intermediate silica levels. They are primarily made of plagioclase feldspars, clinopyroxenes, hornblendes and vitric fragments. Andesitic tempers are found in regions such as Marianas (e.g. tpr21), Bismarcks (e.g. tpr191), Vanuatu (e.g. tpr214), and West Carolines (e.g. tpr53). See Dickinson (2006) pg 39, Strekeisen (2006-2020), or Perkins, (2022) section 6.6.

Dacitic and Rhyolitic

Dacitic rocks are similar to andesitic rocks, but contain higher levels of quartz. Within the Marianas, Saipan is the most likely source of dacitic fabrics (such as tpr5). In the feldspars in dacites are primarily plagioclase and similar rocks with more alkali feldspars are called rhyolitic. Rhyolitic fabrics are found in Haruku, Maluku (e.g. tpr78). See Dickinson (2006) pg 39, Strekeisen (2006-2020) dacite, rhyolite, or Perkins, (2022) section 6.6.

The Pacific fabric types

Dickinson classified the non-calcareous fabrics of the Pacific into 5 main groups: oceanic basalt, andesitic arc, post-arc/back-arc, tectonic highland and dissected orogen.

Oceanic Basalt

The oceanic basalt fabrics of the Pacific, derive from the mantle melt magmas of hot-spot volcanic island chains. They are primarily sourced from alkali olivine basalts or basanites, although in some Samoan fabrics, there is some occurrence of the more felsic lithic varieties, from hawaiite to trachyte. They mainly contain olivines, clinopyroxenes and plagioclase, and have lathwork and mafic vitric lithic fragments. They are only found in East Carolines (e.g. tpr211), Samoa (e.g. tpr294a), Uvea/Rotuma (e.g. tpr328) and the Marquesas (tpr441). See Dickinson (2006) pg 11, 29 or Dickinson (1998) pg 268.

{% include "fabrics2/includes/captionF.html" with sherd="Len4" site="Pohnpei, Carolines" sample="Len4" slide="WD_90_4" num="1094" numf="162" extra="Oceanic basalt fabric from East Carolines." %}

Andesitic Arc

Andesitic arc fabrics are found in the Marianas (e.g. tpr13), Palau (Carolines, tpr53), Bismarcks (e.g. tpr192), Solomons (e.g. tpr111), Vanuatu (e.g. tpr225), Banda (Maluku, tpr203), Kadavu (Fiji, tpr348) and Tonga (e.g. tpr348), where volcanic island arcs were induced by plate subduction. Unlike oceanic basalts, the andesitic arc fabrics can have mineral and rock contributions from mantle sources and the mantle-melt inducing subducting plates. Like the oceanic basalts, they can also have plutonic contributions from intrusive dikes and sills. The tempers are dominated by andesite and basalt lithic fragments, with microlitic, felsitic and vitric textures, and the andesite minerals (plagioclase, clinopyroxene and sometimes hornblende). Quartz and quartz containing dacitic or rhyolytic rocks occur in selected 'andesitic arc' fabrics, along with crystals of orthopyroxene, olivine, oxyhornblende and biotite. See Dickinson (2006) pg 13, 38 or Dickinson (1998) pg 268.

Post-arc and Back-arc

Post-arc and back-arc fabrics come from volcanic eruptions occurring in extinct volcanic arcs, that are not due to plate subduction. Post-arc erruptions occur through rifted island arcs, due to geological events such as tectonic rotation and seafloor spreading. Back-arc erruptions occur through the oceanic crust due to crust spreading. Post-arc tempers are indigenous to the Tabar-Lihir-Tanga-Feni (TLTF) Bismarck island chain (tpr134), Futuna/Alofi (e.g. tpr327) and multiple areas in Fiji (e.g. tpr364). Back-arc tempers are found in Tikopia/Anuta (e.g. tpr244) and the Admiralty Group in the Bismarcks (e.g. tpr94). Clinopyroxenes, plagioclase, and lithics of basalts or shoshonite (a basaltic trachyandesite), dominate both tempers. See Dickinson (2006) pg 13, 75.

Tectonic Highland

Tectonic highland fabrics derive from subducted plate rocks, that have been uplifted and eroded. Uplifting of metasedimentary strata from melanges and underthrust continental crust, has resulted in quartzose fabrics, with chert/metachert and slate/phyllite lithics (including those from Aru, Maluku (tpr63c) and outer Banda Arc, Maluku (tpr80), New Caledonia (e.g. tpr296), and D'Entrecasteaux, PNG. While that from igneous and metamorphic mafic/ultramafic ophiolitic (up-thrusted oceanic basement), has results in variants dominated by clinopyroxene, plagioclase, and metabasaltic and diorite-gabbro plutonic lithics (such as tpr311 in New Caledonia, and 272 in Yap, West Carolines). The ophiolitic variants have diagnostic chrome-spinels and the metavolcanic serpentinite, while the quartzose fabrics have diagnostic amphibolic glaucophane. New Caledonia also has fabrics that are mixed quartzose and ophiolitic tectonic highland (e.g. tpr303). See Dickinson (2006) pg 13, 95 or Dickinson (1998) pg 269.

Dissected Orogen

Dissected orogen fabrics originate from once underground plutonic rocks that have been up-lifted and exposed by erosion. As such they are dominated by plutonic lithics such as microgranite, and those with microphaneritic textures, as well as metavolcanics. They are the only type to have K-feldspars dominate, in addition to plagioclase, and quartz. They are found in PNG (e.g. tpr186), Solomons (e.g. tpr138) and southern Viti Levu, Fiji (e.g. 341a). See Dickinson (2006) pg 13, 87 or Dickinson (1998) pg 269.

Placer

Placer fabrics have higher concentrations of high specific gravity inclusions, such as ferromagnesian silicate minerals such as iron oxides and pyroxenes. Generally these are derived from stream or beach sands manually added to clay. Often sites will show a range of 'placering' in different sherds. For example Teouma, Vanuatu has strongly (tpr228 and tpr229 ), partially(tpr226) and unplacered (tpr225) fabrics. See Dickinson (2006) pg 20 or Dickinson (1998) pg 266.
