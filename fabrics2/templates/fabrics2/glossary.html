Calcareous

Calcarous (calcium carbonate) fabrics include those from reef debri (such as VasinoVA_A1_23) and those from limeclasts (limestone clasts) (such as TTP2_4). While limestones are sedimentary rocks, they were usually included in 'calcareous' by Dickinson, and so have been here. Beach ("white") sand, with the bioclasts ('skeletons') of minute reef organisms (formanifera), was a commonly used temper, and for many Pacific atoll communities it was the only one available. Most Pacific sites feature at least one calcareous or hybrid calcareous temper. Unfortunately this makes reef debri calcareous tempers hard to source to location, although their presence at an inland site is evidence of pottery (or at least temper) importation. In contrast, limeclast fabrics may derive from interior eroded limestones and thus can potentially be more diagnostic. Limeclasts can be distinguished from the bioclasts by their unordered internal structure, although the possibility of degraded bioclasts is a confounding factor. Limeclast fabrics are found in Timor, the Marianas and Vanuatu.

Calcareous inclusions are light grey in both PPL and XPL (higher order colours).

Quartz

Quartz is an important distinguishing mineral in the Pacific, as it only occurs in some tempers, such as those from New Caledonia, Maluku, PNG, Carolines, and Marianas. In the Marianas they are indicative of Saipan, the only Mariana's island with dacitic fabrics.

Quartz minerals are white in PPL and white, grey or extinct (black) in XPL (first order colours). They often have undolose (wavy) extinction, where the extinction travels across the crystal as the polarisation angle changes. Their resistance to alteration/degradation (compared to feldspars) can also be a diagnostic feature.

Igneous Rock Fragments

Igneous rocks are derived from crytallised magma. Igneous rocks are classified by crystal size, which is dependent on magma cooling time. In volcanic rocks, the magma cooled on the earths surface resulting in a aphanitic (very fine) texture. The cateogory traditionally includes vitric (glass) fragments. The colour of the glass varies from brown or red to colourless.

Andesitic

Andesites are igneous (volcanic) rocks that have intermediate silica levels. They are primarily made of plagioclase feldspars, clinopyroxenes, hornblendes and glass.

'Andesitic arc' fabrics are one of the main 'temper' types of the Pacific. They are found in the Marianas, Palau, Bismarcks, Solomons, Vanuatu, Maluku, Fiji and Tonga, where volcanic islands arcs were affected by plate boundary interactions. They are dominated by andesite and basalt lithic fragments and the andesite minerals (plagioclase, clinopyroxenes and sometimes hornblende). Quartz and quartz containing dacitic rocks occur in selected 'andesitic arc' fabrics.

Dacitic

Dacitic rocks are similar to andesitic rocks, but contain higher levels of quartz. Within the Marianas, Saipan is the most likely source of dacitic fabrics.
